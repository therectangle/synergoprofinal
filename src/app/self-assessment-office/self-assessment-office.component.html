Typing habits

CURRENT:

  • Arms kept close to body with wrists rested lightly on desk.

  • Wrists kept straight while typing.

Recommendations

  • Keep your keyboard centered directly in front of you. The letter B on your keyboard will be more or less at your navel.

  • Type with the fleshy part of your fingers rather than just the figner tips. Use a light touch at an even pace. Heavy tapping of the keys at a fast pace can strain the fingers.

  • If you find yourself shrugging your shoulders inwardly while typing, your keyboard might be too small for you. Get a longer one or try a split keyboard.

  • Keep the keyboard as flat as possible. If you can touch type, avoid raising the legs of your keyboard. It just causes you to flex your wrists upwards while typing, causing wrist strain.

  • If you are unable to touch type and are wearing progressive/bi-focal lenses, raising the keyboard legs can help you see your keyboard more clearly. It can help reduce the distance you need to look down, reducing neck strain.

  • Avoid overreaching your fingers or twisting your hands while pressing different keys, it increases finger and wrist strain significantly. E.g stretching out your pinky to press the backspace key.

  • A keyboard wrist rest is only necessary when you are using a thicker keyboard. Most modern keyboards have a slim profile and might cause you to bend your wrists downwards/

  • Avoid using wrist rests constantly while typing, they restrict arm movement and might cause you to flick your wrists and fingers to type. Furthermore, thick wrist rests can compress the nerves in the wrists and cause you to bend your wrists downwards. Rest your wrists on the wrist rest ONLY while between typing tasks.

  • Rest your hands and wrists when not typing. Let go of the keyboard and do not hover your hand over it. Rest your arms on your lap or on your armrests.

Mousing habits

  • Hand fully resting on mouse while mousing.

  • Wrist is bent sideways while mousing.

Recommendations

  • Keep the mouse as close as possible to and inline with the keyboard. You might need a shorter keyboard if you feel that your arm or shoulder is stretched away from your body while mousing.

  • Protect your wrist from compression by keeping it away from any surface. E.g edge of the desk.

  • Avoid using wrist rests while mousing, they restrict arm movement and might cause you to flick your wrist while mousing. Furthermore, thick wrist rests can compress the nerves in the wrists and cause you to bend your wrist downwards.

  • Click with a light touch, try to avoid excessive clicking and mouse movement by using keyboard shortcuts.

  • Be sure to check your computer's mouse settings e.g. mouse tracking/pointer speed if your mouse feels sluggish or non-responsive.

  • Rest your hands and wrists when not mousing. Let go of the mouse and do not hover your hand over it. Rest your arms on your lap or on your armrests.

Lighting

No glare issues.

Recommendations

  • Adjust the screen angle to 90 degrees so that it does not catch reflected light.

  • Adopt the 20-20-20 rule to reduce eye fatigue. Every 20 minutes, look at least 20 feet away for 20 seconds to rest your eyes.

  • Turn on blue light filter/night shift mode on your computer or monitor to reduce blue light exposure.

  • Close the blinds or curtains to cut down glare from sunlight.

  • Adjust screen brightness to match your surroundings, you should not see a reflection when you look at your screen.

Work habits

Taking regular breaks and stretches

Recommendations

  • Frequent micro-breaks (20s-2min) and making use of natural pauses in job activities (e.g rest hands on desk instead of hovering above keyboard when not typing) aid muscle recovery.

  • Regular stretching improves blood circulation and reduces muscle fatigue.

  • Aim to get out of your chair and change posture every hour, avoid holding any posture for too long.

  • Breaks let your muscles recover from fatigue and lets you unwind mentally to regain focus.

Mobile device usage

Holding mobile phone/tablet with one hand.

  • Holding mobile phone/tablet with one hand.

  • Keeping your device at waist level and looking down.

Hazards
  • Hand and wrist pain.

    Holding your device and interacting with one hand can lead to awkward twisting of the hand and wrist. This can strain the tendons and cause pain in the hand and wrists. Most evident while gripping a larger device one handed, causing you to grip the device in a pinch grip.

    Read more
  • Thumb and finger pain.

    Besides affecting our grip, a one handed operation also may cause you to twist your thumbs and fingers awkwardly while tapping on the screen. This too strains the tendons and can cause pain.

    Read more
  • Neck pain

    Keeping the device at waist level brings it far away from us. You would need to bend and look down at your device, increasing neck pressure and possibly long term neck pain.

    Read more
Recommendations

  • Alternate the way you hold your device, regularly switch between a one hand operation and a two hand operation for less hand strain.

  • Alternate tapping between finger and thumb to reduce strain on your thumbs. Using a stylus can eliminate strain.

  • Use a swipe keyboard to type with your finger to reduce strain caused by tapping on the screen.

  • Avoid using the tips of your thumbs to type, they are put at an awkward angle.

  • Grip your devices lightly, relax and keep wrists as straight as possible.

  • A cradle grip can be used to support larger devices and reduce strain. I.e. Left hand supports the tablet from the bottom while right hand taps.

  • Limit device use to 20 minute sessions before taking a break.

  • Use a headset during calls to avoid cradling the phone between your shoulder and neck, reducing neck strain.

  • A wireless headset lets you move while on a call and can reduce back strain by letting you occasionally stand up during long calls.

  • Keep device at chest level to reduce the need to bend and look down at your device.

  • Raising our device also keeps our arms up, this might be tiring but acts as a good reminder to limit device use.

  • Raise your tablet when at a desk and use a wireless keyboard if you plan to do some intensive typing.

  • Turn on blue light/night shift mode to reduce blue light exposure.

Additional tips for your work environment.

Tips for working in break-out area.

Recommendations

  • As break-out areas are designed as sociable and colloborative spaces, they are not intended for long term computer work in general. Avoid working here for more than 30 minutes at a stretch. You might be working in awkard postures without noticing.

  • Casual seating such as bean bag cushions or sofas can be fun and relaxing to sit on but don't really afford long-term back support. It might lead to long-term back pain if you sit and work from them regularly.

  • If you intend to do some intensive computer work, try to at least sit and work from a table.

Additional tips for your work environment.

Tips for working in meeting rooms.

Recommendations

  • Meeting room furniture are generally not designed for long term computer work. The chairs might not be as supportive or adjustable as standard office chairs. Adjust the chair to your comfort and try to stand up and move during long meetings. Wriggle your toes and do some desk stretches if it is not possible to stand up.

  • Apart from attending meetings, try to avoid working in meeting rooms for the long term.

  • If possible, consider holding a short standing meeting or break the rhythm of long meetings by getting everyone to stand up and stretch every hour to create some movement and get the blood pumping.

Seat depth

Discomfort: Your current seat depth might be the cause of your reported lower back discomfort/pain. The following recommendations will help you address this issue.

Seat depth too deep.

Hazards
  • Lower back pain

    The seat edge will create a pressure point on the back of your knees when your seat depth is too deep. This may cause you to sit forward to relieve the pressure but you will lose essential back support. An unsupported back increases low back strain and can lead to long-term back pain.

    Read more
  • Shoulder and upper back pain

    When you sit forward to relieve the pressure, you will lose essential back support. An unsupported back increases back strain and can lead to long-term back pain.

    Read more
Recommendations

  • Check to see if your chair has an adjustable seat depth. Adjust your seat depth to create a 3 fingers gap between the edge of your seat and back of your knees for optimal support of your lower back and thighs while seated.

  • Wrists compressed against edge of desk while typing.

  • Arms reaching away from body while typing.

  • Wrists are twisted sideways while typing.

Hazards
  • Wrist pain and Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

    Compressing the wrists against the desk edge can strain the nerves and tendons in the wrist. If this continues for some time, the median nerve in the forearm gets compressed and may start to swell. The swelling of the median nerve causes Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.

    Read more
  • Hand and finger pain

    As the nerves in the hand pass through at the median nerve. Compression of the median nerve may also cause tingling pain in the hand and fingers.

    Read more
  • Arm and shoulder pain.

    Excessive reaching and extending of your arms to mouse and type can put much strain on your shoulders and arms. Particularly, if you put your whole body weight on your arms and shoulders when you lean forward onto the desk. This may lead to long-term pain if continued.

    Read more
  • Wrist pain and Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

    Twisting or bending the wrist awkwardly can strain the nerves and tendons in the wrist. If this continues for some time, the median nerve in the forearm may start to swell. The swelling of the median nerve causes Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.

    Read more

IDEAL:

Recommendations

  • Place the keyboard such that your wrists do not rest directly on the edge of the desk while typing. This reduces contact stress on the wrists.

  • Your arms and elbows should be by your side when you type, this relaxes the shoulders. Move closer to the desk and place your keyboard and mouse closer if needed. As a side benefit, this encourages you to sit back into your chair.

  • Keep your wrists as straight as possible while typing to reduce wrist strain caused by twisting.

  • Keep your keyboard centered directly in front of you. The letter B on your keyboard will be more or less at your navel.

  • Type with the fleshy part of your fingers rather than just the figner tips. Use a light touch at an even pace. Heavy tapping of the keys at a fast pace can strain the fingers.

  • If you find yourself shrugging your shoulders inwardly while typing, your keyboard might be too small for you. Get a longer one or try a split keyboard.

  • Keep the keyboard as flat as possible. If you can touch type, avoid raising the legs of your keyboard. It just causes you to flex your wrists upwards while typing, causing wrist strain.

  • If you are unable to touch type and are wearing progressive/bi-focal lenses, raising the keyboard legs can help you see your keyboard more clearly. It can help reduce the distance you need to look down, reducing neck strain.
